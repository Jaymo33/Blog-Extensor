<h2>Why accurate flour measurements matter</h2>
<p>Baking is a science, and even small discrepancies in flour measurements can affect your results. Too much flour can make your cakes dense, while too little can leave them flat. Using grams instead of tablespoons eliminates guesswork, especially with self-raising flour, which contains baking powder. Air fryer recipes, like those on <a href=https://www.airfryerrecipe.co.uk/recipes>our recipes page</a>, often require precision for even cooking. Measuring by weight ensures your bakes turn out perfectly every time.</p>

<p>Self-raising flour is a staple in British baking, and its unique composition means it behaves differently to plain flour. The added raising agents mean that the weight per tablespoon can vary slightly. For 0.75 tbsp of self-raising flour, the 5.6-gram measurement is a reliable average. If you're adapting a recipe for your air fryer, this accuracy is key to success.</p>

<h3>How to measure 0.75 tbsp of self-raising flour</h3>
<p>If you don't have a tablespoon measure, you can use a teaspoon—0.75 tbsp is equivalent to 2.25 tsp. For the most accurate results, use digital kitchen scales to weigh out 5.6 grams. This method is particularly useful for air fryer baking, where precision ensures even cooking. For more tips on measuring ingredients, check out <a href=https://www.airfryerrecipe.co.uk/recipe-category/cakes>our cake recipes</a>.</p>

<p>This small quantity of flour is often used in recipes where a light texture is key, such as sponges or batters. In air fryer cooking, it might be used to dust ingredients or thicken sauces. Self-raising flour’s leavening properties make it ideal for quick bakes, like scones or muffins. For inspiration, browse our <a href=https://www.airfryerrecipe.co.uk/recipe-category/breakfast>breakfast recipes</a>.</p>

The 0.75 tbsp measurement might seem small, but it’s often just enough for single servings or testing new recipes. Always store your flour in an airtight container to maintain its quality.</p>
