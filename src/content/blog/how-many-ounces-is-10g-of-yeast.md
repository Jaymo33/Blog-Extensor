<h2>Why accurate yeast measurements matter</h2>  
<p>Yeast is a living organism, and its quantity directly impacts how your dough rises. Too little yeast, and your bread might not rise properly. Too much, and you could end up with an overly yeasty flavour. For air fryer recipes, precise measurements ensure even cooking and perfect texture. If you're converting 10g of yeast to ounces, knowing it's roughly 0.35 oz helps maintain consistency. Check out our <a href=https://www.airfryerrecipe.co.uk/air-fryer-conversion-chart>air fryer conversion chart</a> for more handy measurements.</p>  

<p>Many UK bakers use grams, but some recipes, especially older ones, might list yeast in ounces. Converting 10g of yeast to ounces ensures you're not left guessing. This is particularly useful when scaling recipes up or down. For example, if a recipe calls for 20g of yeast, you'll know that's about 0.7 ounces. Accurate conversions help avoid baking disasters and wasted ingredients.</p>  

<h2>How to measure 10g of yeast without scales</h2>  
<p>Not everyone has a kitchen scale, but there are ways to measure 10g of yeast without one. One standard UK teaspoon holds about 3g of yeast, so 10g would be roughly 3 and a third teaspoons. If you're using ounces, remember that 0.35 oz is just over a third of an ounce. For air fryer recipes, precision matters, so investing in a small digital scale can be a game-changer. <h3>Types of yeast and their weights</h3>  
<p>Active dry yeast, instant yeast, and fresh yeast all have slightly different weights per volume. For example, 10g of fresh yeast isn't the same as 10g of dry yeast in terms of rising power. However, the weight-to-ounce conversion remains the same—10g is always 0.35 oz. If you're adapting a recipe for your air fryer, always check which type of yeast is recommended for best results.</p>
