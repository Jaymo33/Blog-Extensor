<h2>Why Accurate Measurements Matter in Air Fryer Baking</h2>  
<p>Baking in an air fryer requires precision, as even small measurement errors can affect texture and taste. Icing sugar, also known as powdered sugar, is often used in glazes, frostings, and light batters. A slight variation in weight can make your dessert too dense or overly sweet. For example, if you're making air fryer cookies from <a href=https://www.airfryerrecipe.co.uk/recipe-category/cookies>airfryerrecipe.co.uk/recipe-category/cookies</a>, 0.375 cup of icing sugar should weigh 1.5 oz for the best results. Always use a kitchen scale for the most accurate measurement.</p>  

<p>Many air fryer recipes, especially those for cakes and desserts, rely on exact sugar ratios. If you’re converting from cups to ounces, remember that 1 cup of icing sugar is roughly 4 oz. This means 0.375 cup is just under half that weight. For more conversions, check out <a href=https://www.airfryerrecipe.co.uk/air-fryer-conversion-chart>airfryerrecipe.co.uk/air-fryer-conversion-chart</a> to avoid guesswork.</p>  

<h3>How to Measure 0.375 Cup of Icing Sugar Correctly</h3>  
<p>To measure 0.375 cup of icing sugar, use a dry measuring cup and level it off with a straight edge. If you don’t have a 0.375 cup measure, you can use 3/8 cup or combine 1/4 cup plus 2 tablespoons. For the most precise results, a digital kitchen scale is ideal—simply set it to ounces and weigh out 1.5 oz. This method eliminates packing inconsistencies, which can affect your recipe’s outcome.</p>

<p>This measurement is often used in small-batch baking, such as air fryer cupcakes or mini desserts. For example, a light glaze for air fryer cinnamon rolls might call for 0.375 cup (1.5 oz) of icing sugar mixed with milk. <h3>Converting Other Sugar Measurements</h3>
