<h2>Why accurate treacle measurements matter</h2>  
<p>Treacle is a key ingredient in many traditional British desserts, and its sticky consistency means even small measurement errors can affect your dish. Too much treacle can make baked goods overly dense, while too little might leave them dry. When using an air fryer, precise measurements ensure even cooking and perfect texture. For example, a treacle tart or parkin requires the right balance of ingredients to achieve that signature sticky sweetness. Visit <a href=https://www.airfryerrecipe.co.uk/recipes>our recipe section</a> for treacle-based air fryer ideas.</p>  

<p>Measuring treacle by weight (ounces) is often more reliable than volume (cups) because it accounts for density variations. A kitchen scale eliminates guesswork, especially when scaling recipes up or down. If you're adapting a family recipe for the air fryer, converting 3 cups of treacle to ounces ensures consistency. <h3>How to measure treacle without a scale</h3>  
<p>If you don’t have a kitchen scale, use the spoon-and-level method for cups. Lightly grease your measuring cup to help the treacle slide out easily. Fill the cup to the brim, then level it off with a straight edge. Remember, 3 cups of treacle to ounces is roughly 24 oz, but slight variations won’t ruin most recipes. <h2>Common uses for treacle in air fryer cooking</h2>  
<p>Treacle adds depth to both sweet and savoury dishes. In the air fryer, it’s perfect for glazing meats, enhancing roasted vegetables, or creating sticky desserts. A treacle glaze on gammon or a drizzle over roasted carrots caramelises beautifully under the air fryer’s heat.
